There are four ways to think about the future.

Nick Foster, former head of design at Google X, argues that most of our thinking about what comes next falls into one of four mindsets. Each has virtues. Each has blind spots. Today, we're going to use one of them to be provocative.

Could
"What is possible?"
The explorer's mindset. What could exist if constraints were removed? What's technically feasible? The future as an open field.
Should
"What is right?"
The ethicist's mindset. What should we build, given our values? What future are we morally obligated to pursue?
Might
"What is likely?"
The forecaster's mindset. Given current trends and trajectories, what will probably happen? The future as projection.
Don't
"What must we avoid?"
The guardian's mindset. What futures should we actively prevent? What are the risks of inaction?

A few questions to sit with:

Which of these is your default? Which do you reach for first?

Which is hardest for you? Why?

What happens when a team thinks about the future through different lenses at the same time?

Nick Foster, Could Should Might Don't: How We Think About the Future (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2025)

Why It Persisted

Three structural barriers explained
why investment alone hadn't moved the needle

Washington wasn't short on funding, will, or programs. It was short on a mechanism to move a system of 295 independent districts. Here's what was in the way, and what changed.

01
Fragmented
295 independent districts. When a school found something that worked, there was no default mechanism to spread it. What students experienced depended on which building they walked into.
Now: 14 districts have adopted the playbook. The mechanism to spread what works is proof that travels principal-to-principal.
02
Misaligned
No shortage of investment. What was missing was coherence: shared goals, consistent data use, and infrastructure to know whether investments were changing outcomes for kids.
Now: Innovative Schools share NWEA data 3x/year. Rankings are transparent. Schools learn from top performers because the data is open.
03
Outdated
Curriculum, credential pathways, and the definition of "ready" were built for an economy that no longer existed. AI was reshaping every sector. Students growing up near the companies building these tools deserved a pathway to use them.
Now: Every Innovative School has structured AI instruction. Students learn when to use the tools and when to push back.

Spotlight
Agentic AI hit the classroom.
Students didn't just keep up.
They pulled ahead.

In 2028, something shifted. AI stopped being a classroom add-on and became the learning environment itself. Students in Innovative Schools weren't just using AI tools. They were learning alongside AI agents that adapted to how each student thinks, identified gaps in real time, and personalized instruction at a level no single teacher could match alone.

A student with dyslexia at a Tacoma elementary school now works with an AI reading companion that adjusts pacing, font, and comprehension scaffolding in real time. Her reading level jumped two full grades in one year. A high schooler in Highline's early college program used an AI research assistant to co-author a published paper on local water quality. He got a conditional job offer from a biotech firm before graduation.

By 2030, the transformation was undeniable. Students in the network were completing coursework faster, going deeper into subjects they cared about, and arriving at college with skills their professors hadn't seen from incoming freshmen. UW's admissions office started tracking Innovative School graduates as a cohort. Their first-year retention rate was 94%, compared to 78% for similar demographics statewide.

The question colleges started asking wasn't whether these students were ready. It was whether the colleges were ready for them.

In 10 Washington Schools, a Quiet Revolution Is Producing Results That Demand Attention

A network of high-poverty schools is outperforming state averages in math and sending graduates to college at rates their communities haven't seen in a generation.

By Sarah Chen • Education Reporter • 12 min read

When Nik Nair became principal of Lindbergh Middle School in 2028, his building's 8th-grade math proficiency rate was 14%. It is now 31%. That number is not exceptional by itself. What makes it exceptional is where Lindbergh sits: in one of the highest-poverty corridors in King County, serving a student body that is 89% students of color.

Nair is a 2024 Teach For America corps member from SeaTac. He grew up thirty minutes from the school he now leads. He is one of 37 TFA alumni now leading schools across Washington state. And Lindbergh is one of 10 "Innovative Schools" in a network that, five years ago, existed only as an idea in a strategic plan.

"I grew up here. These are my students. We didn't wait for the system to change — we built something real in one building, and then the building next door asked how we did it." Nik Nair, Principal, Lindbergh Middle School (Corps 2024)

The results are turning heads. Across the 10 schools, math proficiency among low-income students has risen an average of 2.3 percentage points per year since 2027, compared to 0.4 points statewide. Reading gains are comparable. And the college enrollment rate for network graduates reached 67% last year, exceeding the state average by 17 points.

Perhaps most notable: every Innovative School now has structured AI instruction built into daily practice. Students aren't just using AI tools. They are learning when not to, building the critical judgment that employers say separates a capable graduate from a dependent one.

Across the hall from Nair's office, Jared Smith — Washington's 2030 Teacher of the Year and a 2024 corps member — runs a 7th-grade math classroom where every student uses AI daily. Down in Renton, Krista McMullen, a 2024 corps member who spent nine years teaching in the South before joining TFA, is now an assistant superintendent driving the district's adoption of the Innovative School model.

In Highline, Ash Ramirez and Joe Hille — both 2025 corps members — launched an innovative early college program that has become the district's fastest-growing pathway. And in Federal Way, Claire Wu and Bradee Parisotto, both from the 2024 corps, built an in-district alumni innovation zone that now connects 40 TFA alumni working across five schools.

"Other states have programs. Washington has proof. That's the difference. You can visit these schools. You can talk to these principals. The results are right there." Dr. Karen Mitchell, Georgetown Center on Education and the Workforce

The question education leaders are starting to ask is not whether the model works. It is whether Washington is ready to scale it.

OSPI Highlights Innovative Schools Network as Model for Statewide Math Recovery
Superintendent cites "credible, replicable proof" in annual address to educators
FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE • January 14, 2031 • Olympia, WA

OLYMPIA — In her annual State of Education address, Superintendent of Public Instruction Dr. Reyes highlighted the Teach For America Washington Innovative Schools Network as a leading example of what she called "the kind of credible, replicable proof our system needs."

The network's 10 schools, all serving high-poverty communities, have posted average annual math proficiency gains of 2.3 percentage points since 2027, nearly six times the state average for comparable schools. College enrollment among graduates reached 67%, exceeding similar demographics by 17 points.

"For two decades, we have invested more per pupil than almost any state," Dr. Reyes said. "What these schools have shown us is that investment works when it is organized around visible results, concentrated talent, and shared accountability. We don't need more programs. We need more proof. And now we have it."

OSPI will include the Innovative School Playbook in recommended resources for districts pursuing math recovery strategies in the 2031-32 school year.
